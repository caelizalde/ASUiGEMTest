Making Arizona Water Safer

Eleminating Microplastics in Our Enviroment

The ASU iGEM team considered many possibilities when searching for a suitable mechanism to break down PFAS. One of the first ideas that we had was incorporating PFAS straight into the cell membrane of a bacteria like E. coli since PFAS has similar characteristics to the lipid-bilayer. We also considered using peroxidases like horse-radish peroxidase and alkane monooxygenase to produce high-energy peroxides that could break down PFAS through radical mechanisms. Ultimately, we decided that both these methods would not be feasible because the first one didnâ€™t solve the problem of PFAS degradation and the second one could have negative effects on the bacterial cell.
We ended up choosing beta oxidation coupled with dehalogenation since we can exploit the fatty acid nature of PFAS like perfluorooctanoic acid (PFOA) or perfluorohexanoic acid (PFHxA) for the degradation. Beta oxidation can break the PFAS into smaller bits which can then be defluorinated by using specific enzymes from the haloacid dehalogenase enzyme superfamily, Therefore, the bacteria can use PFAS as a carbon source and can break down these synthetic chemicals in the process.
